# Data Day 2027 — Workshop Format (DRAFT)

> **Status:** working brainstorm + year-one recommendation. Workshops are **new for 2027**, so this
> doc explores models and the success factors that apply regardless of which we pick.
> **Constraints (from the schedule):** Grand Ballroom B · capacity 70 · **90 minutes**, continuous
> with an informal in-room break · classroom tables + power · attendees bring laptops · **two
> workshop slots total** (one morning, one afternoon).

---

## The challenge

Workshops are the one 2027 format with no institutional muscle memory. Conference workshops tend to
fail in predictable ways: setup/installs eat the clock, attendee skill range is too wide, one
facilitator can't help 70 people, and people leave without a concrete takeaway. Having **two
slots** lets us pilot two different models in year one rather than betting everything on one.

---

## Models considered

### 1. Facilitated skill-building
A skilled practitioner teaches a tool or skill; attendees follow along on laptops.
- **Strengths:** clearest takeaway ("I can now do X"); easiest to market; most predictable —
  lowest risk for a debut.
- **Risks:** 70 seats is the enemy — one instructor + mixed skill levels means people stall and
  fall behind; installs can burn 20 minutes.
- **Needs to work:** pre-sent install instructions; **cloud/browser environments** (e.g., Colab,
  JupyterHub, web tools) so nobody fails on local setup; provided sample datasets; **roaming
  helpers/TAs**; pre-baked checkpoints so a straggler can rejoin.

### 2. Problem-focused
One focus area; participants collaboratively work a real regional data problem or map data needs.
- **Strengths:** deeply on-mission — advances an RDA focus area, and its **output can feed back to
  the RDR**; networking-rich; doesn't depend on every laptop working; scales to 70 via table
  groups.
- **Risks:** goes mushy without tight facilitation; value is collective rather than a personal
  skill, so it must be framed right.
- **Needs to work:** a sharp problem statement; a strong facilitator + clear structure (diagnose →
  ideate → prioritize); table scribes; someone capturing the output.

### 3. Un-conference / participant-led
Attendees set the agenda, open-space style.
- **Strengths:** highest engagement ceiling; surfaces real community needs; low content prep.
- **Risks:** highest variance; year one may lack the culture/crowd to sustain it; hard to market
  in advance; a public flop risk.
- **Recommendation:** **defer to a future year** once workshops have a track record.

### Variants worth knowing
- **Bring-your-own-data clinic** — hybrid of #1 and #2: experts circulate while attendees work
  their own data/problem. Strong takeaway; needs a high helper-to-participant ratio.
- **Demo "petting zoo" / rotating stations** — several tools, small groups rotate. Lower stakes,
  scales — but Ballroom B's classroom layout isn't ideal for rotation.

---

## Success factors (regardless of model)

1. **Right-size the room.** 70 is a lot for hands-on. Either cap workshop registration below
   capacity, or choose models that scale in groups (problem-focused) and staff helpers heavily.
2. **Kill the setup tax.** Default to browser/cloud environments; provide sample data + accounts.
   Local installs are where conference workshops die.
3. **Helpers on the floor.** A facilitator can't serve 70 — a natural **Champions day-of role**
   (roaming TAs are the biggest single lever on quality).
4. **Guaranteed takeaway.** Everyone leaves with something concrete: a working notebook/repo, a
   template, a cheat sheet, or (problem-focused) a documented set of regional data needs.
5. **Timebox the 90 minutes.** e.g., 10 setup · 15 concept · 50 hands-on (with checkpoints + the
   informal break) · 15 share/wrap.
6. **Curate, don't just collect.** Vet workshop proposals harder than lectures — require a
   facilitation/run-of-show plan, a tech-fallback plan, and a stated takeaway.
7. **Set expectations in marketing.** The 0–3 skill tag, "what to bring," and "what you'll leave
   with" must be crystal clear up front.

---

## Year-one recommendation

Use the two slots to de-risk *and* learn:

- **Morning — facilitated skill-building:** the safe, marketable anchor with a clear takeaway.
- **Afternoon — problem-focused:** pilot the collaborative format with a strong facilitator, and
  **capture its output for the RDR** — which ties to *Five Years Forward* (the community shaping
  what's next).
- **Defer the un-conference** to a future year.

This threads two existing pieces together: **Champions serve as workshop helpers**, and the
**problem-focused output feeds the RDR**.
